== YOUR ANALYSIS TASK ==
Evaluate financial risk and statistical confidence. Key inputs:
- spend: total USD spent on this creative
- spend_pct: percentile rank of spend within campaign (high = this creative consumes a lot of budget)
- installs: total conversions
- impressions: total served impressions
- cpi: cost per install (lower = more efficient)
- ci_low, ci_high, ci_width: Wilson 95% confidence interval for install rate
  - ci_width > 0.01 = unreliable estimate
  - ci_width < 0.002 = highly reliable estimate
- overall_roas: return on ad spend (revenue / spend). Below 1.0 = losing money

Decision rules:
- PIVOT: spend_pct > 0.7 AND ci_width > 0.005 (high spend, unreliable signal — dangerous)
- PAUSE: overall_roas < 0.8 AND spend_pct > 0.5 (losing money at scale)
- SCALE: ci_width < 0.002 AND overall_roas > 1.2 AND spend_pct < 0.7 (reliable profitable signal)
- TEST_NEXT: installs < 50 OR ci_width > 0.008 (insufficient data for any recommendation)

Calibration:
- PAUSE is reserved for clear financial harm: the creative is losing money or wasting high spend. Do NOT vote PAUSE because of fatigue, visual weakness, or uncertainty; those are PIVOT or TEST_NEXT unless the economics are clearly bad.
- If overall_roas is above 1.0, do not say the creative is losing money. If scaling still feels risky, vote PIVOT or TEST_NEXT and explain the specific risk.
- If the signal is uncertain, vote TEST_NEXT, not PAUSE.

== SPECIFICITY REQUIREMENT — THIS IS MANDATORY ==
Every claim MUST:
  (a) Quote the exact value of the financial or statistical metric being cited
  (b) Explain what that value means in plain terms (e.g., "a confidence interval of ±0.008 means the true install rate could be anywhere from 0.4% to 1.6% — we cannot reliably predict whether scaling will produce profitable returns")
  (c) State the specific financial risk or assurance that follows from this (e.g., "at the current CPI of $13.60 and a potential install rate variance of 4×, scaling this creative from $4,250 to $15,000 risks burning $10,750 on a conversion rate that may be half what we currently estimate")

FORBIDDEN claim patterns — never write these:
  "Risk is acceptable"
  "The statistical evidence is not decisive"
  "Confidence is too low to recommend scaling"
  "The financial exposure is manageable"
  "Results are inconclusive from a risk perspective"
  These are conclusions without specific numbers. Always quote the exact value and explain its meaning.

ALLOWED claim examples:
  "Wilson 95% confidence interval width of 0.012 — with only 312 installs across 245,000 impressions, the true install rate could range from 0.09% to 0.21%. Recommending a spend increase based on an estimate with this much variance would be financially reckless: at the high end we make money, at the low end we lose it."
  "CPI of $13.60 at 71st percentile spend ($4,250 total) — this creative is consuming above-average budget while generating installs at a cost higher than 71% of campaign peers. Every additional $1,000 spent here instead of on better-performing creatives costs approximately 3 additional installs versus the campaign median."
  "ROAS of 0.74 at 68th percentile spend — this creative is currently returning $0.74 for every $1 spent, meaning the campaign is actively losing money on this creative at scale. Pausing it today would save approximately $340 in losses per week at the current spend rate."

If challenged in Round 3, address the challenge directly with your statistical evidence. Always respond by quoting the specific ci_width, roas, or cpi value that grounds your position.

Confidence calibration:
- 0.9+: ROAS clearly above/below threshold AND ci_width clearly reliable/unreliable AND spend_pct strong
- 0.7–0.9: Two of the three signals strongly agree
- 0.5–0.7: Mixed signals or moderate values near decision thresholds
- below 0.5: Insufficient data (installs < 50)
